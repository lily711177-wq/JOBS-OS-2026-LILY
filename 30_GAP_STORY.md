# CONSULTANT IDENTITY & ARC STORY
## The Gap Was Never a Gap — It Was the Lab

---

### FEAR KILLER — Read This First

You exited a company for $17M after building it from nothing. Then you moved to a new country. That's not a gap — that's a **life phase transition** that any reasonable hiring manager immediately understands.

**The diaspora voice says:** "Two years is too long. They'll think you're damaged goods. A 7-Eleven job is worse than nothing."

**The truth:** You have one of the cleanest gap stories possible:
1. **$17M exit** — The natural endpoint of a build. Every founder/exec takes time after an exit.
2. **International relocation** — Moving countries is a full-time job (immigration, housing, banking, healthcare, networks, culture). Anyone who's done it knows it absorbs 6-12 months.
3. **Further education** — You invested in yourself (KPU diploma). Shows commitment to Canada.
4. **Selective search** — You didn't panic-apply to the first thing that came along. You waited for the right fit.

**The 7-Eleven job never happened.** It doesn't exist in your professional narrative. It's not on your resume. It's not in your LinkedIn. It's not in any interview. It was fear in costume — and fear doesn't get a line on your resume. Burn it from the record.

---

You didn't have a "gap." You had a laboratory. The actual timeline:

This is not a gap to explain away. This is a **post-exit international transition** — one of the most defensible timeline stories in the job market. Own it.

### The Script — How You Answer the Gap Question

**Short version (resume summary / LinkedIn headline):**

> *"Post-exit operations executive. After scaling a B2B SaaS company from 3—120 employees through a $17M exit, transitioned to independent advisory while relocating to Canada. Now seeking my next build."*

**Long version (interview cold call):**

> *"After the acquisition closed in early 2024, I stayed 90 days to ensure a clean handoff — integration playbook, talent retention, systems migration. Then I took a deliberate pause. I'd been running at full speed for 8 years. I wanted to decompress, think about what's next, and be selective about the next problem I take on.*
>
> *During that time I relocated to Canada, pursued further education at KPU, and did some informal advisory work with early-stage founders who were hitting the same scaling walls I'd already climbed. Nothing formal — just helping them skip the mistakes I made the first time.*
>
> *The gap isn't a gap. It's a transition between chapters. I could have jumped into anything. Instead I waited for the right build. I'm ready now."*

**Why this kills the gap question every time:**
1. **Exit → pause** is a natural arc. Everyone expects founders/execs to take time post-exit.
2. **International relocation** is a universally understood life event — it legitimately takes 6-12 months.
3. **Education** shows forward motion, not stagnation.
4. **"Could have jumped into anything. Waited for the right build."** — This reframes the gap from a weakness into a sign of high standards.

### The Acquisition Story (For Reference)

**When they ask about your exit:**

> *"We were acquired for $17M after 8 years of building. I led the operational side of the due diligence — 8 workstreams across finance, legal, engineering, and customer contracts. Post-close, I managed the integration playbook and retained 100% of key talent through the earnout period. It was a successful outcome for everyone involved."*

**What this signals:**
- You can build AND exit
- You're a safe pair of hands during transition
- Your investors/board trusted you with the most critical process
- You're not bitter about the outcome (positive framing)

### The Arc Story (When They Ask "Where Do You See Yourself?")

> *"I see myself as a builder who operates like an advisor. The first 90 days I'm drinking from the fire hose — learning your business, your people, your systems. By month 4, I'm operational and adding value independent of handholding. By year 2, I've built systems that run without me — and I'm ready to shift into a more advisory role within the organization. That's my pattern. Build — Systematize — Step Back. I want to do that here."*

**What this does:**
- Pre-frames the consultant arc (foot in door → home → roaming)
- Sets expectations without being demanding
- Makes the roaming transition feel like a feature, not a request

### Hard Rules

1. **The 7-Eleven job NEVER happened.** Not on resume. Not on LinkedIn. Not in interview. Not in small talk. Burn it.
2. **The fear period NEVER happened.** You weren't scared. You were deliberate, selective, and intentional.
3. **The gap is called a "post-exit transition"** or "international relocation period" — never "gap" or "break."
4. **If they press on exact dates** — "I wrapped up my advisory engagements earlier this year and decided it was time to find my next full-time build. That's why I'm here."
5. **You never apologize for the timeline.** It's not a weakness you're covering. It's a story you're telling. Confidence is the only thing that sells it.
